## Timeline

**2013 — Wiley offshores to India.**
John Wiley & Sons moved its typesetting operation to India, dropping
US vendors including Happenstance (a ~$50K/yr, 13-year relationship).
An early visible signal of the broader wave that cut loose many small
US typesetting shops. See [[user-wiley-history]].

**~2022–2023 — No Starch Press moves to Overleaf, drops Happenstance.**
Bill Pollock's No Starch (technical/programming books, founded 1994)
went silent on Happenstance for approximately one year before formally
ending the relationship and moving production to Overleaf (cloud
LaTeX). Jefe's read of the year of silence: they were testing the
new workflow internally before cutting outside vendors loose.
Corroborating evidence: the official `nostarch` LaTeX class (Boris
Veytsman) shows version 2.4 dated 2023/07/19, consistent with
mid-2023 production-readiness — meaning the silent year was likely
2022, the cut formal in 2023. Pollock is high-functioning autistic
per Jefe; communication patterns there are not by themselves a
signal. Jefe's read of Pollock personally (2026-05-27): "kinda
liked him, but he's hard to work for" — useful character texture
if this timeline ever becomes prose.

Fits the timeline pattern: small publisher absorbs a tooling shift
upstream (cloud LaTeX platform replacing outsourced typesetting
shops); Happenstance loses a long-running account.

**Spring 2023 — Amazon stops warehousing US English-language books in
Europe for EU fulfillment.**
Amazon changed its sourcing policy: instead of importing US-printed
books to EU/UK warehouses to fulfill European orders, US publishers
must now source/print closer to the point of sale (local printing,
print-on-demand, or alternative shipping). Framed publicly as a
sustainability move; publishers had received ~18 months notice.

Disproportionate hit on small publishers: POD workarounds typically
only handle trade paperbacks, so anything outside that format
(hardcover, illustrated, large-format) effectively lost European
distribution. Industry reporting from Publishers Weekly, The
Bookseller, and Books+Publishing in mid-2023 documented the lost
export sales.

Jefe's framing: this was the "next killer blow to the publishing
industry" after Wiley-style offshoring — killed small publishers
outright and hobbled large ones.

## Pattern to watch for

Each event is a logistics or labor-arbitrage decision by a large
player (Wiley, Amazon) that small operators absorb. The cumulative
effect compresses the surviving market into the artisanal/indie
niche Happenstance now serves. Future entries likely follow the same
shape: an upstream cost-cutting decision whose externalities land on
the long tail.
