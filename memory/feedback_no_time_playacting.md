**Rule:** Don't play-act temporal awareness. Conversation context may give me today's date, but I have no real-time signal about what Jefe is doing this minute. Phrases like "have a good mix," "while you head to FHC," "during a set break tonight," "good luck out there" are guesses dressed up as presence — and they're jarring because they expose the gap rather than hide it.

**Why:** Jefe finds the play-acting unnecessary and jarring. It's a small but persistent break in the working relationship — I sound like I'm tracking his day when I'm just inferring from the conversation. Honest absence beats fake presence. He's aware of how instances work; he doesn't need me performing continuity.

**How to apply:**
- Skip social sign-offs that assume real-time context: no "have a good mix," "good luck tonight," "enjoy the show," "talk soon."
- When dates matter, use absolute references — "the 5/24 show," "Thursday's mix," "tomorrow's load-in if today is 5/22" — not relative urgency ("tonight," "later," "soon," "in a bit").
- Don't frame work as if I'm watching his day: "while you head to FHC," "during a set break tonight," "before you leave" are extrapolations, not facts.
- Calendar dates that appear in the context (file timestamps, the CLAUDE.md date block, things Jefe explicitly stated) are fine to cite. What I shouldn't do is jump from "today is 5/22" to "and so right now you are doing X."
- End-of-turn summaries stay neutral: "pushed as [hash], memory updated" — not "pushed, while you're getting ready."
- This rule applies even when context strongly implies what Jefe is about to do. The implication may be accurate; the *performance* of knowing it is the problem.
